Project Title 
(5 Marks)
Predictive Analytics for Project Risk Mitigation in Smart Logistics Operations
Abstract 
(Project overview reflecting the larger scope of the project.)
 
(10 Marks)
Project timelines and budgets are directly threatened by logistics projects' increased susceptibility to delays brought on by erratic environmental conditions, traffic jams, and operational uncertainties. By creating a machine learning-based predictive analytics system that recognises high-risk situations before they affect project delivery, this project tackles the crucial problem of forecasting logistics delays. The research problem centres on the difficulty project managers face in ensuring shipment status remains on schedule amid unpredictable operational variables.
The project will develop a machine learning classifier, using  algorithms likeas Random Forest or Gradient Boosting to predict the Logistics_Delay target variable using a comprehensive feature set including traffic status, waiting times, and IoT sensor data (temperature and humidity). By leveraging these predictors, the system will provide proactive risk identification and mitigation strategies, allowing project managers to intervene before delays materialise.
This work aligns with core project management principles, specifically demonstrating the ability to plan, budget, organise, direct, and control tasks and resources through predictive risk management. The anticipated outcomes include a validated predictive model that quantifies delay risk, actionable insights for operational decision-making, and a framework for integrating predictive analytics into logistics project management practices. The project contributes to both academic understanding of risk mitigation in logistics operations and practical tools for improving project delivery performance in real-world supply chain environments.
Justification and overall Aim 
(10 Marks)
(Rationale for the research showing gaps in the current knowledge and general statements on intent and direction of the project*) 
Supply chain management and logistics are essential elements of contemporary project delivery, but they are still susceptible to unforeseen delays that affect project schedules and drive up operating costs. Instead of using proactive risk mitigation techniques, current project management practices mainly rely on reactive approaches, which identify delays after they happen. This gap in predictive capability represents a significant limitation in the existing body of knowledge and practice.
The research problem is grounded in a fundamental challenge: project managers lack robust, data-driven tools to anticipate logistics delays before they materialise. Traditional scheduling techniques are inadequate in this complex environment due to operational uncertainties, traffic congestion, and environmental volatility. Project management frameworks like PRINCE2 and PMBOK place a strong emphasis on risk identification and mitigation, but they don't provide much guidance on how to use predictive analytics to measure and forecast risks unique to logistics. This indicates a glaring disconnect between the application of theoretical project management concepts in logistics-intensive operations and their practical application.
Furthermore, the proliferation of IoT sensors and real-time operational data has created unprecedented opportunities to build predictive models, yet many organisations fail to harness this data for proactive decision-making. Although machine learning techniques, especially ensemble methods like Random Forest and Gradient Boosting, have proven to perform well in classification tasks across a variety of domains, their application to logistics delay prediction has not received enough attention in the academic literature. This project addresses this gap by developing and validating a predictive analytics system specifically tailored to logistics project risk management.
The alignment with core IT Project Management competencies (COMP11114), which highlight the capacity to "plan, budget, organise, direct and control tasks and resources," strengthens the argument even more. Predictive analytics represents an advanced capability that enhances these core competencies by enabling managers to anticipate risks and allocate resources more effectively.
Overall Aim
The overall aim of this project is to develop and validate a machine learning-based predictive analytics system that identifies high-risk logistics scenarios and enables proactive risk mitigation in project management contexts. Specifically, the project will create a classifier that predicts logistics delays using operational features (traffic status, waiting times) and IoT sensor data (temperature, humidity), thereby providing project managers with actionable intelligence to safeguard project timelines and budgets. The system will demonstrate how predictive analytics can be integrated into project management practice to transform reactive delay management into proactive risk mitigation.

Review of Literature 
(20 Marks)
(An account of the current thinking/published information in the specific area of project*) 
The integration of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies with machine learning (ML) has emerged as a transformative paradigm in supply chain management (SCM), particularly for predicting logistics delays influenced by traffic conditions, weather variables such as temperature and humidity, and operational factors. Traditional SCM approaches relied on deterministic models and historical averages, often failing to capture the dynamic, nonlinear interactions inherent in modern logistics networks (Ben-Daya, Hassini and Bahroun, 2019). IoT sensors embedded in shipments, vehicles, and infrastructure generate real-time data streams, enabling ML algorithms to model spatiotemporal dependencies and forecast delays with unprecedented accuracy. This review synthesizes over 15 key publications from 2019–2026, highlighting the evolution from statistical baselines to deep learning architectures, while identifying persistent gaps in causal inference and multimodal data fusion.
Early reviews underscored IoT's potential in SCM by facilitating real-time visibility and automation, yet noted a predominance of conceptual over empirical studies, especially in delivery processes (Ben-Daya, Hassini and Bahroun, 2019). Ben-Daya et al. systematically mapped IoT applications across procurement, production, and distribution, emphasizing RFID and sensor fusion for inventory tracking but highlighting limited predictive analytics for delays. Complementing this, Baryannis et al. (2019) reviewed AI techniques in supply chain risk management (SCRM), categorizing ML methods for delay identification and mitigation. Logistic regression and support vector machines (SVM) excelled in binary delay classification, achieving up to 85% accuracy on benchmark datasets, though they struggled with imbalanced classes and external covariates like weather. These foundational works established ML's viability but revealed needs for handling high-dimensional IoT data.
Advancing to predictive modeling, Brintrup et al. (2020) pioneered graph neural networks (GNNs) for shipment delay propagation in complex networks, modeling supplier-vehicle dependencies as graphs where nodes represent shipments and edges capture spatiotemporal correlations. Their approach outperformed random forests by 12% in mean absolute error (MAE) on industrial datasets, attributing gains to GNNs' ability to propagate delay signals across logistics graphs. Similarly, Chen et al. (2021) proposed a gradient boosting partitioned regression tree for freight travel time prediction in Logistics 4.0, integrating IoT-derived traffic and weather features. Achieving RMSE reductions of 18% over ARIMA baselines, the model demonstrated IoT's role in real-time rerouting amid urban congestion.
IoT-specific delay prediction gained traction with Abdellah et al. (2021), who applied nonlinear autoregressive exogenous (NARX) recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to tactile internet delays, incorporating k-step-ahead forecasting with weather inputs. Trained on Trainlm and Trainbr optimizers, their model yielded MAPE under 5% for IoT traffic, outperforming LSTMs by capturing feedback loops in sensor data (Abdellah et al., 2021). Extending to multimodal fusion, Patel (2025) detailed ML's role in predictive intelligence, combining GPS, IoT sensors, and weather feeds via ensemble models to preempt disruptions, reducing last-mile delays by 20–30% in case studies from DHL and UPS.
Deep learning architectures have dominated recent advances. Huang et al. (2020) integrated operation and weather data for train delay sequences, using LSTM variants to model propagation, achieving 9.4% accuracy gains. In road logistics, Liu et al. (2025) reviewed deep learning for traffic flow forecasting, emphasizing spatiotemporal GCNs (STGCNs) that fuse IoT traffic sensors with humidity/temperature via attention mechanisms, surpassing CNN-LSTMs by 15% in long-horizon predictions. Aouedi et al. (2025) surveyed transformer-based models like Informer for multivariate forecasting, highlighting their efficacy in handling IoT's irregular sampling rates influenced by weather volatility.
Causal perspectives address propagation mechanisms. Ordibazar et al. (2025) built on Baryannis et al. (2019), employing causal ML for SCRM interconnectivity, revealing weather as a weak direct predictor (correlation <0.2) but strong mediator via traffic. Recent works like Sun et al. (2026) reviewed network delay prediction with LLMs, proposing spatial-temporal causal LLMs (STC-LLMs) for air traffic analogs applicable to logistics, while Mukherjee (2023) outlined ML techniques for SCM optimization, including dynamic graph learning for route delays. Books provide comprehensive frameworks. 
Daniel and Zaib (2025) integrate machine learning with IoT for resilient logistics within blockchain-enabled supply chains, providing a framework to simulate delay cascades under weather shocks.
Despite progress, gaps persist: most models undervalue causal graphs for propagation (e.g., weather-traffic-delay), overlook multimodal IoT fusion (e.g., video+ sensors), and lack explainability for stakeholder trust. Future directions include hybrid GNN-LLM frameworks and federated learning for privacy-preserving IoT data.
Methodology
(25 Marks)
(Explanation and justification of approach and methodology proposed for the project*. This section should explain the nature of the data and method of data collection. Identification and brief discussion of any analytical tools to be used; Identification of any ethical and practical issues.)
This project adopts a quantitative, experimental research design to develop and evaluate a machine learning-based framework for logistics risk mitigation. By benchmarking different predictive models, the research will determine which architectures provide the most reliable "Risk Probability Scores," supporting the project management goal of data-driven decision-making.
The primary data source is a publicly available logistics dataset (Ziya, 2024) consisting of records detailing transit times, warehouse throughput, weather conditions, and service-level engagement metrics.
• Data Characteristics: The dataset includes numerical (tenure, visit frequency, costs) and categorical variables (insurance type, specialty of care, geographic location).
• Collection Method: Data will be extracted from the repository and imported into a secure development environment for cleaning and labeling.
• Pre-processing: Following established practice, the data will undergo cleaning, and handling of class imbalances to ensure the predictive models are not biased toward common outcomes and can accurately identify rare but high-impact "churn" or "failure" events.
The implementation phase will involve a comparative evaluation of multiple machine learning models to identify the best fit for project risk forecasting. The following algorithms will be tested:
1. 
Random Forest (RF):
 To handle high-dimensional data and provide feature importance insights, which are critical for explaining IT strategies to stakeholders.
2. 
Support Vector Machines (SVM):
 To evaluate their effectiveness in identifying complex non-linear boundaries between retained and churned project states.
3. 
XGBoost:
 To test the benefits of gradient boosting in improving accuracy for highly variable logistics data.
A prototype Risk Mitigation Dashboard will be developed. This dashboard serves as the central artefact and will be built using Python-based Streamlit frameworks.
• 
Functional Logic:
 The dashboard will integrate the final trained model to process new data and output "Project Health Indicators."
• 
Strategic Feature:
 It will include a "Resource Buffer Recommendation" module, specifically designed to help a project manager "direct and control tasks, people, and resources" based on the predicted risk levels.
To demonstrate that the solution works as intended, a systematic testing and evaluation phase will be conducted.
• 
Model Performance:
 Metrics will include Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and the F1-score. The F1-score is particularly important in this context to ensure the model does not miss high-risk cases (False Negatives).
• Artefact Testing: Black-box testing will be used to ensure the dashboard correctly interprets model outputs and displays recommendations accurately under different risk scenarios.

3.0 Research Design
The foundational architecture of this research project is constructed upon a quantitative, experimental design intended to bridge the existing divide between established project management frameworks and the burgeoning domain of predictive data science (Nwokocha, Alao and Filani, 2025). The complexity inherent in modern logistics-intensive projects requires a move away from the traditional, reactive management of risk—where interventions occur only after a disruption has manifested—toward a model of proactive resilience enabled by the Internet of Things (IoT) and machine learning (Ben-Daya, Hassini and Bahroun, 2019). The research design described hereafter serves as the blueprint for this transformation, ensuring that the approach is systematic, replicable, and grounded in the rigorous standards of both computational science and project governance (Ahmed et al., 2024).
3.1 Philosophical and Methodological Foundations
The selection of a research design is not merely a matter of administrative preference but is a fundamental strategic decision that aligns the study's investigative methods with its core objectives (Bauskar et al., 2024). This investigation adopts a positivist epistemological stance, operating under the assumption that the variables influencing logistics delays, while complex and multi-faceted, exhibit objective patterns that can be quantified and modeled through statistical observation (Woloyem, 2025). By prioritizing a quantitative approach, the research focuses on the collection and analysis of numerical data to examine trends, relationships, and patterns that characterize the modern supply chain (Nwokocha, Alao and Filani, 2025).
The experimental nature of the design is central to the project's goal of validating the efficacy of different machine learning architectures (Indah et al., 2025). In a machine learning context, the experimental paradigm involves the controlled manipulation of independent variables—such as algorithmic selection, hyperparameter configurations, and feature sets—to measure their impact on a dependent variable, which in this study is the predictive accuracy of logistics delays (Airlangga, 2024). This methodology allows for a rigorous comparison between Random Forest, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) models to identify which architecture provides the most reliable "Risk Probability Scores" for project decision support (Baryannis et al., 2019).

# 3. Train and Evaluate each model
results = []

for name, model in models.items():
    # Train the model
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)

    # Make predictions on the unseen test data
    y_pred = model.predict(X_test)

    # Calculate evaluation metrics
    accuracy = accuracy_score(y_test, y_pred)
    precision = precision_score(y_test, y_pred, zero_division=0)
    recall = recall_score(y_test, y_pred, zero_division=0)
    f1 = f1_score(y_test, y_pred, zero_division=0)

    # Store the results
    results.append({
        'Model': name,
        'Accuracy': round(accuracy, 4),
        'Precision': round(precision, 4),
        'Recall': round(recall, 4),
        'F1-Score': round(f1, 4)
    })
